📐 Grammar

Grammar carries significant marks in NIOS English. Focus: Tenses, Voice, Narration, Modals, Transformation. These are asked every year.

📅 Tenses
G1 Fill in the blanks with the correct form of the verb given in brackets:
(a) She ______ (go) to school every day.
(b) They ______ (finish) the project by next Monday.
(c) I ______ (wait) for him since morning.
(d) He ______ (not/eat) anything since yesterday. PYQGRAM ▼
✎ Model Answer
  • (a) goes — Simple Present (habitual action)
  • (b) will have finished — Future Perfect
  • (c) have been waiting — Present Perfect Continuous
  • (d) has not eaten — Present Perfect
For "since/for" → use Perfect or Perfect Continuous. "Since" = point of time; "for" = duration.
G2 Fill in the blanks using appropriate tense:
(a) By the time we reached the station, the train ______ (leave).
(b) The sun ______ (rise) in the east.
(c) She ______ (study) for three hours when the power went off.
(d) If I had money, I ______ (buy) a new phone. 🔥 HOTGRAM ▼
✎ Model Answer
  • (a) had left — Past Perfect (action completed before another past action)
  • (b) rises — Simple Present (universal truth)
  • (c) had been studying — Past Perfect Continuous
  • (d) would buy — Conditional (Type 2)
Universal truths, scientific facts → always Simple Present regardless of context.
🔄 Active / Passive Voice
G3 Change the following sentences into Passive Voice:
(a) She writes a letter.
(b) The teacher punished the student.
(c) They are building a new hospital.
(d) You must follow the rules.
(e) Who has broken this glass? PYQGRAM ▼
✎ Model Answer
  • (a) A letter is written by her.
  • (b) The student was punished by the teacher.
  • (c) A new hospital is being built by them.
  • (d) The rules must be followed (by you).
  • (e) By whom has this glass been broken?
Formula: Object + helping verb (be-form) + V3 + by + Subject. Modals in passive: Modal + be + V3.
G4 Change to Active Voice:
(a) The book was read by him.
(b) A song is being sung by Meena.
(c) The match has been won by India.
(d) Let the door be opened. 🔥 HOTGRAM ▼
✎ Model Answer
  • (a) He read the book.
  • (b) Meena is singing a song.
  • (c) India has won the match.
  • (d) Open the door. / Let him open the door.
💬 Direct / Indirect Speech
G5 Change into Indirect Speech:
(a) She said, "I am going to Delhi."
(b) He said to me, "Do you know the answer?"
(c) The teacher said, "The earth revolves around the sun."
(d) She said, "Please help me."
(e) He said, "What a beautiful painting!" PYQGRAM ▼
✎ Model Answer
  • (a) She said that she was going to Delhi.
  • (b) He asked me if/whether I knew the answer.
  • (c) The teacher said that the earth revolves around the sun. (No tense change for universal truth)
  • (d) She requested me to help her.
  • (e) He exclaimed that it was a very beautiful painting.
Tense shift rule: is→was, am→was, are→were, will→would, can→could, may→might. Pronouns shift: I→he/she, we→they, you→I/he/she.
G6 Change into Direct Speech:
(a) He told me that he had finished his homework.
(b) She asked him if he was coming to the party.
(c) The teacher ordered the students to be silent. 🔥 HOTGRAM ▼
✎ Model Answer
  • (a) He said to me, "I have finished my homework."
  • (b) She said to him, "Are you coming to the party?"
  • (c) The teacher said to the students, "Be silent."
📌 Articles & Determiners
G7 Fill in the blanks with a/an/the or leave blank (—):
(a) I saw __ elephant at __ zoo.
(b) She is __ honest woman.
(c) He plays __ guitar every evening.
(d) __ Ganga is __ sacred river.
(e) __ gold is __ precious metal.
(f) He is __ best student in the class. PYQGRAM ▼
✎ Model Answer
  • (a) an, the
  • (b) an (vowel sound /ɒ/)
  • (c) the (musical instruments take "the")
  • (d) The, a
  • (e) — (material nouns in general sense), a
  • (f) the (superlative → always "the")
Key rules: rivers/mountains/oceans → "the"; musical instruments → "the"; superlatives → "the"; first mention → a/an; second mention → the.
⚙️ Modals

  • (d) should / ought to

🔁 Sentence Transformation
G9 Transform the sentences as directed:
(a) He is too weak to walk. (Remove 'too')
(b) No other student is as intelligent as Riya. (Change to Comparative)
(c) He said, "How cold it is!" (Exclamatory to Assertive)
(d) Work hard or you will fail. (Use 'If')
(e) As soon as she entered, everyone stood up. (Use 'No sooner') PYQ🔥 HOTGRAM ▼
✎ Model Answer
  • (a) He is so weak that he cannot walk.
  • (b) Riya is more intelligent than any other student.
  • (c) It is very cold.
  • (d) If you do not work hard, you will fail.
  • (e) No sooner did she enter than everyone stood up.
too…to → so…that…not | No sooner → inverted structure: No sooner + had/did + Subject + V1/V3 + than…
📍 Prepositions
G10 Fill in the blanks with appropriate prepositions:
(a) She is afraid __ spiders.
(b) He is good __ mathematics.
(c) The meeting will start __ 9 a.m. __ Monday.
(d) She has been living here __ 2018.
(e) I congratulated him __ his success. PYQGRAM ▼
✎ Model Answer
  • (a) afraid of
  • (b) good at
  • (c) at 9 a.m. on Monday
  • (d) since 2018 (point of time)
  • (e) congratulated him on his success
Common pairs: good at, interested in, afraid of, proud of, congratulate on, at (time/exact), on (day/date), in (month/year).
✏️ Error Correction
G11 Identify and correct one error in each sentence:
(a) She don't know the answer.
(b) The informations given are incorrect.
(c) He is more smarter than his brother.
(d) I have seen that movie yesterday.
(e) Neither of the boys have done their work. PYQ🔥 HOTGRAM ▼
✎ Corrections
  • (a) She doesn't know the answer. (Subject-verb agreement: she → doesn't)
  • (b) The information given is incorrect. (Uncountable noun)
  • (c) He is smarter than his brother. (Remove double comparative)
  • (d) I saw that movie yesterday. (Past Simple with past time expression, not Present Perfect)
  • (e) Neither of the boys has done his work. (Neither → singular verb)

📖 Reading Comprehension

Two passages are typically given — one factual/expository and one literary. Answers must come from the passage. Learn the technique: underline keywords in the question, locate in passage, paraphrase.

Passage 1 — Factual / Expository
C1 Read the following passage and answer the questions. [Practice Passage — Typical PYQ Style] PYQ🔥 HOT ▼
✎ Passage + Questions + Answers

Water is the most precious natural resource on earth. All forms of life depend on it. Despite covering more than 70% of the Earth's surface, only about 3% of the world's water is fresh water, and most of that is locked in glaciers and ice caps. This makes fresh water an increasingly scarce and valuable resource.

The overuse of water for agriculture, which accounts for about 70% of global fresh water consumption, is a major concern. Industrial processes and domestic use also put enormous pressure on water supplies. Climate change is further aggravating the situation by causing irregular rainfall patterns and accelerating the melting of glaciers.

Conservation of water is therefore a necessity, not a choice. Simple measures like rainwater harvesting, using drip irrigation in agriculture, fixing leaking taps, and recycling water can make a significant difference. Governments must invest in water management infrastructure, and individuals must cultivate habits of mindful water use. A world without adequate clean water is a world in crisis.

Questions and Model Answers:
  • Q1. What percentage of Earth's surface is covered by water?
    Ans: More than 70% of the Earth's surface is covered by water.
  • Q2. What percentage of fresh water is available for use?
    Ans: Only about 3% of the world's water is fresh water, and most of it is locked in glaciers.
  • Q3. How is climate change affecting the water situation?
    Ans: Climate change is aggravating the water crisis by causing irregular rainfall patterns and accelerating the melting of glaciers.
  • Q4. Mention any two measures to conserve water.
    Ans: Rainwater harvesting and using drip irrigation in agriculture are two effective methods of water conservation.
  • Q5. Find a word in the passage which means 'making worse'.
    Ans: Aggravating (Paragraph 2)
Vocabulary questions: always look in the paragraph mentioned. Re-read one sentence before and after. Comprehension answers should paraphrase — don't copy word-for-word.
Passage 2 — Literary / Narrative
C2 Read the passage and answer the questions. [Literary Passage — PYQ Style] 🔥 HOTIMP ▼
✎ Passage + Questions + Answers

Grandmother was a very old woman. Her face was a criss-cross of wrinkles, her hair was white, and she walked with a slight stoop. But there was an energy in her movements that belied her age. She had been a great beauty in her youth — at least, that is what she often said, and we never had any reason to doubt her.

Every morning, she would sit in the sun in the courtyard and feed the sparrows. She knew each one of them personally, or so she believed. The sparrows would perch on her hands, her shoulders, and even on her head, and she would laugh with delight. It was the high point of her day.

She died one winter morning, sitting in the sun in the courtyard. The sparrows came as usual, but she could no longer feed them. We scattered breadcrumbs for them. They ate quietly, and then they flew away — and somehow, that small, ordinary act seemed like a farewell from the birds she had loved so dearly.

Questions and Model Answers:
  • Q1. Describe the grandmother's appearance.
    Ans: The grandmother was very old, with a wrinkled face, white hair, and a slight stoop. Despite her age, she had an energetic way of moving.
  • Q2. What was the "high point of her day"?
    Ans: Feeding the sparrows every morning in the courtyard was the high point of her day. She believed she knew each sparrow personally and delighted in their company.
  • Q3. How does the author suggest the sparrows also mourned the grandmother?
    Ans: After she died, the sparrows ate the breadcrumbs scattered for them quietly and then flew away. The narrator says this felt like a farewell from the birds who loved her — suggesting even they sensed her absence.
  • Q4. Find a word in Paragraph 1 that means 'contradicted / went against'.
    Ans: Belied
C3 Technique Q: What are the key strategies for answering Reading Comprehension in NIOS English? How do you handle vocabulary (word-meaning) questions? IMP ▼
✎ Exam Strategy
  • Read the questions first — then read the passage (saves time)
  • Underline keywords in questions, locate their position in the passage
  • Answers are always in the passage — never add your own opinion
  • Paraphrase — don't copy sentences verbatim; show you understood
  • Word-meaning/synonym questions: Look in the paragraph mentioned. Often the surrounding sentence gives clues.
  • Title questions: Choose a title that covers the entire passage, not just one part
  • Marks = sentences. 2-mark answer = 2 clear points. Don't write essays.
📚 Literature — Prose

These questions are from the NIOS English Reader. Match with your prescribed texts. Themes and character questions are most frequently asked.

The Last Lesson — Alphonse Daudet
L1 What was the "last lesson" about? What made that day's lesson different from any other day? PYQLIT🔥 HOTSHORT ▼
✎ Model Answer
The "last lesson" was the final French language class taught by M. Hamel in a village in Alsace after the Prussian conquest. The Prussians had decreed that henceforth only German would be taught in the schools of Alsace and Lorraine. What made that day different was the extraordinary atmosphere. M. Hamel wore his fine Sunday clothes, the village elders sat at the back of the class, and everyone felt a deep sense of grief and regret. Franz, who used to dread his French lessons, suddenly found himself loving the language. M. Hamel spoke movingly about the importance of their mother tongue, calling French the most beautiful, clearest, and most logical language in the world. At the end of the lesson, when the church bell struck noon, M. Hamel was overcome with emotion and could only write "Vive La France!" on the board before dismissing the class.
Key themes to remember: nationalism, importance of mother tongue, loss, regret, love for one's language and identity.
L2 Draw the character sketch of M. Hamel as portrayed in "The Last Lesson". OR What qualities does M. Hamel display on the day of the last lesson? PYQLITLONG ▼
✎ Model Answer
M. Hamel is the French teacher at the village school in Alsace and the central figure of Alphonse Daudet's "The Last Lesson." He is a complex character who embodies both human weakness and noble dedication. Under ordinary circumstances, M. Hamel was a strict teacher — he would punish students who had not done their homework and sometimes sent them to water his flowers. Franz had often dreaded his class. However, on the day of the last lesson, a remarkable transformation is visible in him. He arrives in his finest clothes — his beautiful green coat, frilled shirt, and embroidered cap — as a mark of respect for the occasion. He is patient, gentle, and deeply emotional. He blames himself and the parents for having sometimes sent children to work instead of school. He speaks with passionate love about the French language, calling it the most beautiful tongue in the world. Most importantly, he embodies patriotism — his devotion to French language and culture represents love for the fatherland. At the end, overcome with grief and unable to speak, he writes "Vive La France!" on the board. In those final moments, M. Hamel transforms from a schoolteacher into a symbol of national identity and resistance.
L3 What was Franz's attitude towards his French lessons at the beginning of the story? How did it change by the end? What caused this change? 🔥 HOTLITSHORT ▼
✎ Model Answer
At the beginning of the story, Franz has a careless and indifferent attitude towards his French lessons. He has not learnt his lesson on participles and is afraid of being scolded. He prefers playing outdoors and finds his lessons dull and burdensome. By the end of the story, however, Franz undergoes a complete transformation. He begins to love and value the French language deeply. He regrets having wasted his time, not paying attention in class, and not learning properly. The cause of this change is the announcement that it would be the "last lesson" in French. The realization that he would never again have the chance to learn his mother tongue makes him value what he had neglected. The emotional atmosphere, M. Hamel's dignified conduct, and the presence of the village elders all contribute to his awakening. The lesson teaches him — and the reader — that we often value things only when we are about to lose them.
L4 What is the central theme / message of "The Last Lesson"? What lesson does Alphonse Daudet convey to the reader? PYQLIT🔥 HOT ▼
✎ Model Answer
The central theme of "The Last Lesson" is the importance of one's mother tongue as an inseparable part of national identity and cultural survival. Through the story, Daudet conveys that language is not merely a means of communication — it is the soul of a people. When a nation loses its language, it loses its identity. The story also explores the themes of loss, regret, and patriotism. Both Franz and the villagers realize the value of their language only when they are about to lose it forever. This serves as a universal lesson: we often neglect what is most precious — our culture, language, and heritage — until it is taken from us. M. Hamel's statement that French is the most beautiful language in the world, and his passionate plea to the students to guard it well, reminds us that preserving one's language is an act of patriotism and self-respect.
A Hero — R.K. Narayan
L5 Why was Swami afraid at first to sleep alone? How did his father's challenge change him? PYQLIT🔥 HOT ▼
✎ Model Answer
Swaminathan (Swami) was a young boy who was used to sleeping beside his grandmother. He was afraid to sleep alone in the office room because the dark corner near the bureau scared him and he feared supernatural presences. The very idea of spending the night alone filled him with terror. His father challenged his courage after Swami expressed fear, telling him that only cowards were afraid of the dark and that a boy his age should be able to sleep anywhere. Stung by his father's words and unwilling to be called a coward, Swami reluctantly went to sleep alone. That night, Swami heard a noise in the dark and, in a panic, bit hard into what he thought was a ghost's leg. It turned out to be a burglar who had entered the house. Swami had accidentally caught and exposed a thief! The following day, he was celebrated as a hero by the police, his family, and the school. The story shows that circumstances and necessity can bring out courage even from the most unexpected sources. Swami's fear was real, but facing it — however accidentally — transformed him into a hero and built his confidence.
The Address — Marga Minco
L6 Why did the narrator go to Mrs Dorling's house? Why did she decide not to go back a second time? PYQLITSHORT ▼
✎ Model Answer
The narrator went to Mrs Dorling's house after the war to recover the household belongings that her mother had entrusted to Mrs Dorling for safekeeping. Her mother had given away everything — silver cutlery, antiques, linen — to Mrs Dorling before the family was deported. However, when the narrator finally entered the house, the familiar objects from her childhood home — the tablecloth, the cutlery, the candlesticks — seemed utterly out of place in the ugly, cluttered surroundings of Mrs Dorling's house. Seeing them brought back overwhelming memories of her mother and the life that had been destroyed by the war. The objects, instead of being comforting, became a painful reminder of everything she had lost. She decided not to go back a second time because she realised that the past could not be recovered — the objects meant nothing without the people and the life they were once part of. She chose to let go of the material possessions to free herself from the grief and the burden of memory.
Key theme: The story explores memory, loss, displacement, and the impossibility of recovering the past after the Holocaust.
🎭 Literature — Poetry
My Mother at Sixty-Six — Kamala Das
P1 What is the central idea of the poem "My Mother at Sixty-Six" by Kamala Das? What emotions does the poet experience during the drive? PYQLIT🔥 HOTLONG ▼
✎ Model Answer
The central idea of "My Mother at Sixty-Six" is the fear of separation and loss that a daughter experiences when she sees her ageing mother's declining health. The poem explores the universal human emotion of confronting the mortality of a loved one. During the drive to Cochin airport, the poet observes her mother dozing beside her. Her mother's pale, ashen, corpse-like face fills her with a familiar, childhood ache — the fear of losing her. She compares her mother's face to "late winter's moon" — pale, dull, and fading. To escape this painful thought, the poet looks outside at the young trees sprinting past and merry children spilling out of homes — images of youth, life, and joy — contrasting sharply with her mother's frailty. At the airport, before parting, she looks at her mother's face once more and, instead of expressing her grief, smiles and says "see you soon, Amma." This "smile and smile and smile" represents a brave, composed farewell masking her inner anguish — her hope that her mother will still be alive when she returns, and her inability to voice her deep fear of this being their last goodbye.
Key images: mother's face = "late winter's moon" / "ashen like a corpse". Young trees = life, vitality, contrast. The repeated "smile" = suppressed grief, false hope, maternal love.
P2 Explain the significance of the images of "young trees sprinting" and "merry children spilling" in "My Mother at Sixty-Six". What do they contrast with? 🔥 HOTLITSHORT ▼
✎ Model Answer
The images of "young trees sprinting" and "merry children spilling out of their homes" are symbols of life, youth, energy, and joy. They represent vitality and a future full of possibilities. These images sharply contrast with the image of the poet's aged mother — pale-faced, corpse-like, and slowly fading. The contrast highlights the cruel gap between the vibrancy of youth and the decline of old age. The poet uses these images to escape momentarily from the painful reality of her mother's mortality. By turning her gaze outward to the living, moving world, she attempts to suppress her fear and anguish. The juxtaposition intensifies the poem's central theme of loss and the inevitability of ageing and death.
Keeping Quiet — Pablo Neruda
P3 What does Pablo Neruda want people to do in the poem "Keeping Quiet"? What would be the result of this stillness? PYQLIT🔥 HOT ▼
✎ Model Answer
In "Keeping Quiet," Pablo Neruda urges all of humanity to observe a moment of complete stillness and silence — to stop all activity, stop speaking, and simply exist together without movement or urgency. He wants people to stop their engines, put down their weapons, stop wars, cease the destruction of nature, and pause from the relentless rush of modern life. He wants fishermen to stop threatening the whales, salt gatherers to stop working, and those preparing wars to put on clean clothes and walk about with their brothers — suggesting an end to violence and conflict. Neruda believes that this moment of stillness and togetherness would allow people to reflect deeply, understand one another, and recognise the futility of war, hatred, and exploitation. It would give the Earth a chance to heal and give human beings a chance to reconnect with themselves and with nature. Crucially, Neruda clarifies that this is not about death or permanent inactivity — it is about "life" and self-reflection. The poem ends with the image of the Earth, which appears dead in winter but secretly keeps life alive beneath the soil — suggesting that silence and stillness can be creative and restorative, not destructive.
Key themes: anti-war, introspection, ecological concern, universal brotherhood, the value of silence. The poem is NOT about death — it is about stillness that leads to awareness and life.
A Thing of Beauty — John Keats
P4 "A thing of beauty is a joy forever." What does Keats mean by this? What things of beauty does he mention in the poem? PYQLIT🔥 HOT ▼
✎ Model Answer
By the famous opening line "A thing of beauty is a joy forever," Keats means that anything that is genuinely beautiful provides lasting pleasure and happiness. Its beauty never fades — it leaves an enduring impression on the mind. Even when the beautiful object is gone, the joy it gave remains forever. It acts as "a bower quiet for us" — a shelter from the sorrows and suffering of life. In the poem, Keats enumerates several things of beauty that give joy: • The sun, the moon, old and new trees that provide shade • Simple daffodils with their green covering • Clear streams and forests amidst which the green world lives • The lovely tales we have heard or read — heroic myths and legends • The mighty dead — great heroes and noble ancestors whose stories inspire us • All lovely things on Earth Keats ends the poem by saying that all these beautiful things flow to us like an "endless fountain" poured from heaven to Earth, showering us with a "spiritual wine" — connecting beauty to divinity and eternal spiritual nourishment. For Keats, beauty and truth are inseparable, and beauty is a source of eternal spiritual sustenance.
An Elementary School Classroom in a Slum — Stephen Spender
P5 What is the condition of the children in the slum school as described by Spender? What does the poet demand for them? 🔥 HOTLITLONG ▼
✎ Model Answer
Stephen Spender paints a deeply disturbing picture of the slum children in the poem. They are thin, pale, and malnourished — their faces are like "rootless weeds," their hair is "slag heap," and they are "stunted" and "twisted." One boy has a "rat's eyes" while another is "bespectacled" with a "paper-seeming boy." They are trapped in lives of poverty with no access to the wider, beautiful world. The classroom walls have maps, pictures of Shakespeare, the Tyrolese valley, and Dante — images of a cultured world — but these are cruel mockeries for children who are imprisoned in slums. The world on the walls represents freedom and opportunity that these children will never have; it is not their world. Spender demands that society — represented by "governor, inspector, visitor" — act urgently to change the lives of these children. He wants them to be given real education, not cosmetic schooling. He demands that the barriers of class and poverty be broken, that these children be allowed to leave their "slag heap" world and access the same sunlit fields, rivers, and history that the privileged enjoy. The poem is a powerful indictment of social inequality and a passionate plea for equal access to education and opportunity. Only when knowledge opens doors for the poor will history truly be rewritten, Spender suggests.
